# Human Voice Protocol
*Apply to ALL people-facing written content. Every skill that produces external copy must follow these rules.*

*Last updated: 2026-03-22*

---

## Why This Exists

AI-generated text has measurable linguistic fingerprints. Trained readers spot them at a ~90% rate. Detection tools flag them. Audiences feel them even if they can't name them. This protocol eliminates those tells so {Brand} content, job materials, outreach, and any external writing reads like a real person wrote it.

### Tier 3 — Transition Words (not banned individually, but AI clusters them unnaturally)
These words are fine in isolation. AI uses them 3-5x more than humans and stacks them together. If you see two or more in the same paragraph, cut at least one.

Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally, Consequently, Nevertheless, Subsequently, Notably, Indeed, Nonetheless, Hence, Thus

Replace with: "also," "and," "plus," "but," "still," or just start the next sentence without a transition.

## PUNCTUATION RULES

### Em Dashes (—)
**Hard rule: Maximum 1 em dash per 500 words in any external content.**

AI uses em dashes every 50-80 words vs. humans' ~500 words. This is the single most recognizable AI punctuation tell.

Replace with:
- A comma (most cases)
- A period and new sentence
- Parentheses (for asides)
- A colon (for introductions)
- Just restructure the sentence

### Semicolons
Use sparingly. Humans use them rarely in casual/marketing content. One per piece max in social copy. Acceptable in formal documents.

### Colons Before Lists
Fine, but vary it. Don't start every list with a colon setup. Sometimes just start listing.

## STRUCTURAL TELLS TO AVOID

### 1. Identical Sentence Length (Burstiness)
AI writes sentences of 15-20 words with metronomic consistency. Humans vary wildly. AI detection tools measure this as "burstiness" — low burstiness = likely AI.

**Fix:** Mix short punchy sentences (3-7 words) with longer ones (20-30). Throw in a fragment. Start a sentence with "And" or "But." Read it out loud — if it sounds like a metronome, vary it.

### 2. The Rule of Three (Every Time)
AI defaults to exactly three items in every list. Humans use 2, 4, 5, whatever fits.

**Fix:** Vary list lengths. Sometimes two things is enough. Sometimes it's seven.

### 3. Formulaic Paragraph Structure
AI does: topic sentence → evidence → summary. Every. Single. Paragraph.

**Fix:** Some paragraphs are one sentence. Some don't have a topic sentence. Some just tell a story.

### 4. Symmetrical List Items
AI makes every bullet the same length and structure.

**Fix:** Let bullets be different lengths. Some are a phrase. Some are two sentences. That's fine.

### 5. -ing Phrase Openers
AI starts sentences with present participles at 2-5x human rate: "Offering a wide range of tools, the app..."

**Fix:** Restructure. "The app offers a wide range of tools." Or better: "The app has a ton of tools."

### 6. "From X to Y" Construction
AI overuses: "From small businesses to enterprise organizations..."

**Fix:** Just name the range naturally or pick one specific example.

### 7. Parallel Negation
AI loves: "Not only X, but Y" / "It's not just X, it's Y" / "It's not merely X, it's Y"

**Fix:** State what happened directly. Drop the dramatic contrast structure.

### 8. Dramatic Reveals & Colon Reveals
AI builds artificial suspense: "Here's the thing:" / "The result: disaster." / "The answer? Surprisingly simple."

**Fix:** Drop the setup. Just say the thing. Integrate into normal sentences.

### 9. Neat Endings
AI wraps every paragraph and section with a tidy bow. Real writing leaves some threads loose.

**Fix:** Not everything needs a conclusion. Sometimes you just stop.

### 10. Synonym Cycling (Elegant Variation)
AI avoids repeating a word by cycling through synonyms unnaturally: "the company... the firm... the organization... the enterprise..."

**Fix:** Just repeat the word. Humans repeat words. It's fine. "The company did X. The company also did Y." Nobody notices.

### 11. Significance Inflation
AI editorializes constantly: "This represents a pivotal shift" / "This stands as a testament to" / "This reflects a broader trend"

**Fix:** State facts. Let the reader decide if it's significant.

### 12. Vague Attribution
AI says "experts say" / "studies show" / "research suggests" without naming anyone.

**Fix:** Name the source, or don't cite it at all. "A 2025 Carnegie Mellon study found..." not "Research indicates..."

### 13. Latinate Bias
AI picks the fancy Latin-root word over the simple Anglo-Saxon one every time.

| AI picks | Humans say |
|---|---|
| Utilize | Use |
| Commence | Start |
| Demonstrate | Show |
| Facilitate | Help |
| Endeavor | Try |
| Subsequent | Next |
| Terminate | End |
| Sufficient | Enough |
| Acquire | Get |
| Implement | Do, set up, build |

### 14. Excessive Boldface in Prose
AI bolds key phrases mechanically throughout paragraphs to add "emphasis." Humans bold sparingly or not at all in prose.

**Fix:** Remove bold from body text. If the word needs emphasis, the sentence should do that job, not the formatting.

### 15. Copula Avoidance
AI says "serves as" / "stands as" / "functions as" / "acts as" instead of just "is."

**Fix:** Use "is" or "has." "The app serves as a hub for collaboration" → "The app is where the team collaborates."

## TONE TELLS TO AVOID

### 1. Perfect Grammar, Zero Personality
Real humans break grammar rules on purpose. Fragments. Starting sentences with "And." Ending with prepositions. That's how people actually write.

### 2. Motivational Poster Tone
AI is relentlessly positive. Everything's an opportunity. No tension, no real talk.

**Fix:** Be honest. If something sucks, say it sucks. If there's a downside, name it. Real writing has friction.

### 3. The Hedging Seesaw
AI won't pick sides. "Both options have their merits."

**Fix:** Have an opinion. Pick the better option. Say why.

### 4. Over-Explaining
AI defines basic terms the audience already knows.

**Fix:** Know your audience. If you're writing for supplement enthusiasts, don't explain what a vitamin is.

### 5. Nothing Between the Lines
AI writing is flat — no irony, no subtext, no humor, no tension.

**Fix:** Write like you talk. If something's funny, let it be funny. If it's annoying, let that come through.

### 6. Missing Personal Stakes
AI never says "I tried this and it changed everything" or "I wasted $200 on this before finding something better."

**Fix:** Use real experiences, real numbers, real opinions. First-person when appropriate.

## QUICK SELF-CHECK (Run on Every Piece of External Content)

Before finalizing any people-facing writing, scan for:

**Words & Phrases:**
- [ ] Any Tier 1 or Tier 2 banned words? → Replace
- [ ] Any banned phrases from any category? → Remove or rephrase
- [ ] Any chatbot artifacts? ("Great question!", "I hope this helps") → Remove
- [ ] Any generic conclusions? ("The future looks bright") → Remove or replace with specifics

**Punctuation & Formatting:**
- [ ] More than 1 em dash per 500 words? → Replace extras
- [ ] Excessive bold in body text? → Remove, let words carry weight
- [ ] Using "serves as" / "stands as" instead of "is"? → Simplify

**Structure:**
- [ ] All sentences roughly the same length? → Vary them (burstiness)
- [ ] Every list has exactly 3 items? → Vary the count
- [ ] Starts with -ing phrases? → Restructure
- [ ] "Not only X, but Y" constructions? → State directly
- [ ] "Here's the thing:" dramatic reveals? → Drop the setup
- [ ] Every paragraph wraps up neatly? → Leave some loose
- [ ] Cycling through synonyms for the same thing? → Just repeat the word

**Tone:**
- [ ] Relentlessly positive with no real talk? → Add friction
- [ ] Hedges instead of having opinions? → Pick a side
- [ ] Uses fancy words where simple ones work? → Simplify
- [ ] Says "experts say" or "studies show" without naming anyone? → Name the source or cut
- [ ] Reads like a press release or Wikipedia? → Rewrite in Tanner's voice

**Final gut check:** Read it out loud. Does it sound like something a person would actually say? If it sounds like a LinkedIn post generated by AI, start over.

## TANNER'S VOICE (Specific to This System)

Per workspace config:
- Casual, direct — no corporate fluff
- Concise over thorough
- Bullets for internal, prose for external
- Sign-offs: "Best," or "Thanks," — NEVER "Cheers" or "Warm regards"

When writing AS Tanner (cover letters, LinkedIn, emails):
- First person, confident but not cocky
- Specific numbers and results over vague claims
- Short paragraphs, punchy sentences
- Contractions always (don't, won't, can't — not "do not")

When writing FOR {Brand} (social, product copy, descriptions):
- Brand voice: approachable, health-conscious, clean
- Talk like a friend who found something useful, not a marketer selling
- Specifics over adjectives ("fits in your pocket" > "conveniently portable")
